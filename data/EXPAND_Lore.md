# PART ONE: OVERVIEW OF THE WORLD

## The Setting

The world of **EXPAND** does not have a proper name anymore. Those who remember what it was called before the Unmaking refuse to speak it — they say the name was swallowed by the dark, and to speak it again would invite the dark back in. Most inhabitants simply call it **the Remnant**, or, in older dialects, **the Hollow Earth**.

The Remnant is a world caught in the slow process of erasure. For centuries, the Cult of Shadows has been performing a rite they call **the Grand Expansion** — a metaphysical unmaking of the physical world that peels back reality like the skin of a fruit, exposing the wet, writhing darkness beneath. Great swaths of land have already been consumed, replaced by vast territories known as **the Unmade** — regions where the laws of reality no longer apply, where shadows move with intent, and where the creatures that dwell there were once human.

What remains of civilization clings to a handful of **Anchor Cities** — places protected by ancient wards called **Stillstones**, giant obelisks of pale limestone etched with sigils of containment. The largest of these cities is **Cerevane**, a walled metropolis built atop a massive Stillstone formation, its towers rising like fingers from a clenched fist. Beyond Cerevane lie the fractured city-states of **Holm**, **Ashenveil**, and **Duskport**, each holding on by varying degrees of desperation and ingenuity.

Between the Anchor Cities stretch **the Woods of Unmake** — vast, warped forests where trees grow in impossible geometries, where sunlight arrives wrong (too red, too cold, at the wrong angle), and where the monsters that were born from the Expansion roam freely.

### Core Themes

- **Entropy as an active force.** The darkness is not passive. It hungers, reaches, and plans.
- **The cost of knowledge.** Many of the setting's most powerful figures gained their power by learning things they cannot unlearn.
- **Survival against inevitability.** The Remnant is losing ground. The question is not whether the world ends — it is whether anything worth saving survives.
- **Moral ambiguity.** The Cult of Shadows does not see itself as villainous. Its members believe they are midwives of a new existence, not destroyers.

# PART TWO: THE HISTORY OF THE CULT OF SHADOWS

## Origins — The Age of the Cartographers (circa 800 years before present)

The Cult of Shadows did not begin as a cult. It began as a **scholarly society** called the **Order of the Outer Meridian**, founded in the city-state of Ashenveil by a natural philosopher named **Sereveth Dawn**. Sereveth was obsessed with a single question: *what lies beyond the edge of the world?*

At the time, maps of the Remnant ended at natural boundaries — the Unmeasured Sea to the west, the Ashen Peaks to the north, the Thornwall to the east, and the Silence (a vast, featureless desert where no sound could be heard) to the south. Sereveth believed these were not natural boundaries but *constructed* ones — deliberate limits placed on the world by some ancient, forgotten power.

She was correct. But she was wrong about the nature of that power.

Her research led her to a set of texts called the **Margins** — documents that predated every known civilization, written in a script that was not quite any known language but could, with effort, be partially decoded. The Margins described the world as a *sealed vessel* — a container of existence held shut against the pressure of something vast and formless outside. The seals, the Margins explained, were called **the Stillstones**, and they had been placed there by an ancient society of godlike beings called the **Founders**.

Sereveth published her findings. Most scholars dismissed them as mystical nonsense.

Three of her students did not.

---

## The First Schism — The Disciples of the Outer Dark (circa 750 years before present)

After Sereveth's death (from natural causes, though later Cult mythology would claim she was *absorbed* into the Dark willingly), her three most devoted students — **Orren Pale**, **Maska of the Two Faces**, and a woman known only as **the Seamstress** — took her research and began working toward a conclusion Sereveth herself had never dared approach: *what if the seals should be broken?*

The three disciples founded a secret society that would become the earliest recognizable form of the Cult of Shadows. They called it **the Order of Expansion**, borrowing the term from a single passage in the Margins that read: *"When the vessel cracks, the dark will expand, and all things sealed inside will be known at last."*

Their early practices were primarily intellectual — decoding more of the Margins, searching for the locations of additional Stillstones, and conducting small experiments in what they called **shadow-threading**: the practice of reaching through the hairline fractures in reality (which they believed existed near old Stillstones) to touch, briefly, the darkness on the other side.

Shadow-threading was immediately, demonstrably dangerous. Orren Pale lost his right hand to it. Maska of the Two Faces returned from a particularly deep threading session with a second face grown on the back of her skull — a face that whispered in a language no one else could understand. The Seamstress disappeared entirely for three months and returned with no memory of the time, but with the ability to unmake small objects by pressing her hands against them.

Despite these costs — or perhaps because of them — the three disciples continued.

---

## The First Expansion — The Shattering of Lorne's Gate (circa 700 years before present)

The city of **Lorne** no longer exists. Where it once stood, at the eastern edge of the known world near the Thornwall, there is now a region of absolute Unmade territory called **the Scar** — a wound in reality approximately thirty miles across where the ground is glass-black, the sky is the color of a bruise, and nothing living can survive for more than a few hours.

The Shattering of Lorne's Gate was the Cult's first major action. Working through a network of agents embedded in Lorne's civic government, Orren Pale arranged for the city's primary Stillstone — a formation called **Lorne's Gate** — to be undermined. The Stillstone was not destroyed directly (the Founders had made them effectively indestructible) but was *tilted* — shifted off its foundational axis by careful tunneling beneath it, disrupting the sigil network carved into its base.

The result was catastrophic. The containment field around the Thornwall collapsed over a period of three days, during which the city of Lorne experienced what survivors described as *"the dark falling inward"* — shadows detaching from surfaces and moving independently, people and objects dissolving at the edges, sound becoming unreliable.

On the third night, Lorne simply ceased to exist.

The Cult of Shadows, as it would come to be known, was born in that darkness. Orren Pale — now calling himself **the First Voice** — declared the Shattering a success and began actively recruiting, drawing in those who had witnessed the event and been changed by proximity to the Expansion rather than destroyed by it.

---

## The Middle Period — Growth and the Grand Design (circa 700–200 years before present)

Over the following five centuries, the Cult grew from a fringe society into one of the most powerful — and most feared — organizations in the Remnant. This period is characterized by two parallel developments: the Cult's expansion of its membership and infrastructure, and its ongoing refinement of the **Grand Design** — the systematic, planned dismantling of the Stillstone network.

Key developments in this period include:

**The Codification of Shadow-Threading** — By approximately 600 years before present, the Cult had developed shadow-threading into a formal magical discipline. Practitioners called **Weavers** could pierce the membrane between the Remnant and the Outer Dark intentionally and consistently, drawing through fragments of darkness that could be shaped into tools, weapons, or servants. The cost — always — was some piece of the Weaver's self. Memory, sensation, identity: the darkness took something personal in exchange for every gift.

**The Founding of the Hollow Council** — Approximately 500 years before present, the Cult's leadership formalized into a governing body called the **Hollow Council**, comprising seven senior Weavers. The Council created the Cult's internal hierarchy (Listener, Shade, Weaver, Voice, and the singular **First Mouth**) and began systematically acquiring political influence in the Anchor Cities.

**The War of Ash** — Between approximately 400 and 350 years before present, the Cerevane military conducted a major campaign against the Cult following the discovery of a Cult cell operating within the city's own government. The War of Ash lasted fifty years, devastated both sides, and ended inconclusively — Cerevane could not destroy the Cult, and the Cult could not yet crack Cerevane's Stillstone. The war, however, radicalized the Cult significantly. Before the War of Ash, some factions within the Hollow Council had argued for gradual, quiet infiltration. After it, the Council unified behind a policy of aggressive expansion.

**The Unmade Forest** — Approximately 300 years before present, the Cult successfully Unmade the first large forest region, creating the prototype for what would later become the entire Woods of Unmake. This was done not through a Stillstone disruption but through a new technique: planting **shadow seeds** — crystallized fragments of the Outer Dark — in the earth of the forest floor and allowing them to slowly corrupt the land from below over a period of decades. The creatures that now inhabit the Woods of Unmake are, in many cases, the descendants of animals and humans who were caught in that original Unmaking.

---

## The Recent Age — The Accelerating Expansion (circa 200 years before present to present)

The last two centuries have seen the Cult's Grand Design accelerate dramatically. Three more Stillstones have been disrupted. Two more cities have been lost. The Unmade territory now covers approximately a third of the known Remnant, and the pace of Expansion is increasing.

The current leader of the Cult is the **First Mouth**, a figure known as **Veleth the Unfinished** — a Weaver of extraordinary power who has been shadow-threading since childhood and who has, over decades of practice, lost so much of herself to the darkness that she is no longer entirely human. She is described by those who have seen her as *"a woman-shaped hole in the world, standing in a woman's clothing."* Her face reportedly changes, her voice carries harmonics no human throat should produce, and she casts no shadow at all.

Under Veleth, the Cult has shifted from patience to urgency. She has declared the **Final Expansion** — the last phase of the Grand Design — to be within a generation's reach. Only three Stillstones remain functional enough to matter. Cerevane's stone is one of them.

The Cult's current activities in the Remnant include:

- **Deep infiltration of Cerevane's civic and religious institutions**, with the goal of creating the same conditions that preceded the Shattering of Lorne's Gate.
- **Active management of the Woods of Unmake**, using Weavers to accelerate the spread of shadow seeds and encourage the breeding of Unmade creatures.
- **Suppression of any research** into counter-measures, Stillstone reinforcement, or the original texts of the Founders.
- **The Harvest** — a program of deliberately exposing people near the Unmade territories to low-level shadow-threading, creating *Changed* individuals who are not fully Unmade but who carry fragments of darkness in them, making them easier to recruit, control, or use as unwitting agents.

---

## Cult Theology and Self-Perception

The Cult of Shadows does not consider itself evil. This is perhaps the most unsettling thing about it.

Cult theology, as codified in a text called **the Book of the Outer Shore**, holds that:

1. The Remnant is a *prison*, not a home. The Founders who created the Stillstones were not protectors but jailers, sealing living beings inside a finite container to serve purposes that were never explained to those contained.
2. The Outer Dark is not emptiness or oblivion but a **vast, formless existence** — a state of being without boundaries, pain, individuality, or death. To be Unmade is not to be destroyed; it is to be *freed* from the illusion of selfhood and merged with something infinite.
3. The Grand Expansion is therefore an act of liberation. Every person who is Unmade has been released from suffering. Every Stillstone that falls is another door opened in the prison wall.

Most Cult members genuinely believe this. Low-ranking members (Listeners and Shades) often came to the Cult during moments of profound grief or trauma, attracted by a theology that reframes loss as release. Senior Weavers have seen enough of the Outer Dark to have their certainty shaken — but by the time they reach senior rank, they have given too much of themselves to the shadow to turn back.

# PART THREE: THE WOODS OF UNMAKE — 10 MONSTERS

The Woods of Unmake are the most dangerous inhabited region of the Remnant outside the Scar itself. They stretch in a vast, irregular band between the last Anchor Cities, and travelers who must cross them do so quickly, in groups, and with as much light as they can carry.

The creatures that live there were not created. They *became*. The Unmaking works on flesh the same way it works on stone and soil — it does not destroy, it transforms, bending living things toward shapes that serve the Outer Dark.

---

## 1. The Hollowmen

**Classification:** Unmade Humanoid
**Threat Level:** High
**Behavior:** Pack hunters

**Appearance:** Hollowmen were once human. They stand upright, they have two arms and two legs, and from a distance, in bad light, they can pass for a person. Up close, the differences become horrifying. Their torsos are hollow — literally empty, the ribs spread wide like the staves of a barrel, the internal organs gone, replaced by a dense, writhing darkness that can be glimpsed through the gaps. Their eyes are uniformly black, and they move with an uncanny stillness between steps, as if each movement is a discrete decision rather than a fluid motion.

**Behavior:** Hollowmen hunt in groups of three to eight. Their primary strategy is deception — they will position themselves at the edge of firelight or at a bend in a path, mimicking distress signals (cries for help, limping gaits, raised hands) to draw travelers close. Once within arm's reach of their target, the darkness inside their torsos extends outward in thin, fast tendrils.

**Abilities:** A Hollowman's touch induces **shadow-numbness** — a creeping paralysis that starts at the point of contact and moves toward the heart. A character touched by a Hollowman must act quickly or find themselves unable to move within minutes. A Hollowman that gets its tendrils around a victim's chest can begin the process of Unmaking — pulling the warmth and memory out of a person while they watch.

**Weakness:** Hollowmen are sensitive to fire and to direct, sustained light. They avoid both instinctively. A bright enough light source will cause them to retreat entirely.

**Lore Note:** Some Hollowmen retain fragments of memory from their human lives. It is possible, rarely, to distract or unsettle one by invoking something from its past — a name, a place, a phrase. This buys seconds at best.

---

## 2. The Ashwalkers

**Classification:** Unmade Animal (former deer or large herbivore)
**Threat Level:** Medium-High
**Behavior:** Territorial solitary wanderers

**Appearance:** An Ashwalker is the size of a large elk, but the resemblance to any natural animal ends there. Its body is covered in a layer of grey ash that constantly sloughs off and regenerates, leaving a faint trail wherever it walks. Its legs are too long, bending at three joints instead of two, and it moves with a stilted, deliberate gait that makes almost no sound. Its head carries no eyes — the sockets are smooth and sealed — but a wide, lipless mouth runs from ear to ear, filled with flat teeth the color of old bone.

**Behavior:** Ashwalkers do not hunt actively. They wander in large, slow circuits through their territory. The danger they present is incidental — their ash is a mild Unmaking agent. Anything that breathes it in large quantities begins to experience **memory dissolution**: recent memories fade first, then older ones, until a person is standing in a forest with no idea who they are or why they came there.

**Abilities:** Area-of-effect ash cloud generation (passive). An Ashwalker that feels threatened will **exhale forcefully**, producing a dense cloud of ash in a wide cone. Direct contact with the cloud causes accelerated memory loss and disorientation.

**Weakness:** Ashwalkers are passive unless cornered. They can be avoided by moving downwind. Their ash does not penetrate water, so a soaked cloth over the mouth and nose provides meaningful protection.

**Lore Note:** Hunters who have tracked Ashwalkers report that the circuits they walk seem to be deliberate patterns — some have interpreted them as a kind of writing, as if the Ashwalker is slowly inscribing a message on the landscape in ash.

---

## 3. The Knot

**Classification:** Unmade Fungal/Organic Mass
**Threat Level:** Very High
**Behavior:** Ambush predator, sessile until triggered

**Appearance:** A Knot looks, at first glance, like a large tangle of roots or dead wood — a roughly spherical mass of interwoven fibrous matter approximately two to three meters in diameter, usually found wedged in the fork of a large tree or half-buried in the forest floor. On closer inspection, the fibers are not wood — they are something between muscle and bark, and they pulse faintly, as if something inside is breathing.

**Behavior:** A Knot remains completely motionless until a warm-blooded creature comes within approximately four meters, at which point it attacks with explosive speed. The fibrous exterior unspools, revealing a hollow interior — a darkness so complete it seems to absorb the light around it — and the fibers reach out to entangle everything nearby.

**Abilities:** **Entanglement** (the fibers are immensely strong and sticky), **shadow-interior pull** (anything pulled into the hollow center is subjected to rapid, aggressive Unmaking — entering the interior is almost certainly fatal), and **silence field** (a Knot emits no sound and suppresses nearby sounds, making it extremely difficult to detect before it is too late).

**Weakness:** The fibers, despite their strength, are highly flammable. Fire will cause a Knot to retreat and unravel back to its resting state. A Knot cannot move faster than a running person once its fibers are fully extended, so creating distance after the initial attack is possible.

**Lore Note:** Cult Weavers are said to use Knots as guardians, planting them at the entrances to underground installations. The Cult has a way of commanding Knots — a specific harmonic tone produced by shadow-threading — that keeps them dormant on command.

---

## 4. The Pale Choir

**Classification:** Unmade Human (spiritual/incorporeal)
**Threat Level:** High (psychological)
**Behavior:** Collective entity, pursues isolated individuals

**Appearance:** The Pale Choir appears as a cluster of translucent humanoid figures — anywhere from six to thirty in a given manifestation — that move together, always within arm's length of each other, and always singing. The song has no melody that can be identified and no words in any known language, but it is deeply, viscerally upsetting to hear, producing in listeners a sensation described as *"being watched from inside my own skull."*

**Behavior:** The Pale Choir does not have a body. It is drawn to isolation — specifically to individuals who are alone and frightened. It will follow a solitary traveler at a distance, just visible through the trees, singing quietly, until the target either finds companions or breaks down completely.

**Abilities:** **Sanity erosion** — extended exposure to the Choir's song causes increasing psychological deterioration: first anxiety, then hallucination, then a catatonic state that the Choir calls *joining*. A person who fully *joins* the Choir loses their individual consciousness and becomes another translucent figure in the cluster. **Mirror speech** — the Choir can produce the voices of people the target knows, usually in the context of that person saying something deeply cruel or deeply frightening.

**Weakness:** The Pale Choir disperses instantly in the presence of genuine community — it cannot approach a group of three or more people who trust each other. It is also repelled by any object associated with strong personal memory, held and spoken over aloud.

**Lore Note:** Scholars in Cerevane believe the Pale Choir is the remnant of an entire village's worth of people who were Unmade simultaneously — that the individual minds survived in a shattered, merged form, endlessly singing a song that was, once, a lullaby.

---

## 5. The Grinning Wood

**Classification:** Unmade Plant (trees)
**Threat Level:** Medium (environmental)
**Behavior:** Passive corruption, reactive entrapment

**Appearance:** From a distance, a grove of Grinning Wood looks like an unusually dark stand of trees — perhaps just a heavy shadow. From within, the wrongness is immediately apparent. The bark of each tree is split in a long vertical line from root to crown, the split pried slightly open in a way that resembles, unmistakably, a mouth. Inside the split is a faint, dull light — not warm, not white, but the color of old teeth — and the interior is lined with something that looks disturbingly like a tongue.

**Behavior:** Grinning Wood is not aggressive. It does not pursue. But it *lures* — the groves produce a scent that is, to most people, exactly the scent of something they desperately want: a childhood home, a lost love, a meal from a better time. The scent is powerful and deeply personal (the Wood seems to read desires directly from nearby minds) and can draw a person off a safe path without them realizing it.

**Abilities:** **Desire scent** (personalized lure), **root snare** (anyone who stands within a Grinning Wood grove for more than a few minutes will find the surface roots beginning to shift around their feet, slowly and silently), and **whispered offers** — the Grinning Wood can approximate human speech in a low whisper, and will use this to make specific, detailed offers to people standing within it. The offers are always for something real and wanted. The price is never stated.

**Weakness:** A person aware of the Grinning Wood's nature can resist the scent lure with concentrated effort. The roots are shallow and easily cut. The whispering stops entirely if you walk away.

**Lore Note:** The Cult tends its Grinning Wood groves carefully, viewing them as a kind of garden. Groves near Cult installations are cultivated to be especially potent.

---

## 6. The Twice-Dead

**Classification:** Unmade Undead (animated corpse, modified)
**Threat Level:** Medium
**Behavior:** Compulsive wandering, memory-driven

**Appearance:** The Twice-Dead are the bodies of people who died within the Woods of Unmake and were then touched by the slow creep of shadow-threads through the soil. They are not zombies in any classical sense — they do not hunger, they do not attack on sight, and they retain the physical appearance of their living selves almost exactly, with one exception: their shadows move independently of their bodies, sometimes dramatically so, twisting into shapes that have nothing to do with the position of the corpse.

**Behavior:** The Twice-Dead are driven by a single compulsion: they are trying to complete something they left unfinished in life. A woman who died before she could warn her children walks the same route from the woods to a village that no longer exists. A soldier who fell in ambush stands in a clearing, over and over, attempting to reload a crossbow he no longer carries. They will walk through or past travelers without acknowledging them — unless the traveler is associated, by scent or appearance or sound, with whatever the Twice-Dead was trying to reach.

**Abilities:** **Grief aura** — being near a Twice-Dead produces a profound, sourceless sadness that can make it difficult to act decisively. **Shadow-hand** — if a Twice-Dead is prevented from completing its compulsion, the animated shadow will act independently, reaching out to grab or strike. **Memory bleed** — touching a Twice-Dead allows a character to experience a fragment of the person's last memories, which may contain useful information but is deeply traumatic.

**Weakness:** Completing — or meaningfully interrupting and acknowledging — the Twice-Dead's unfinished task will cause it to stop and, gradually, dissolve. Each one is a puzzle in this sense.

## 7. The Hunger Below

**Classification:** Unmade Subterranean Entity
**Threat Level:** Extreme (environmental)
**Behavior:** Ambush predator, underground

**Appearance:** No one has seen the Hunger Below clearly and survived to describe it in full. What witnesses report is a disturbance of the earth — the ground in the Woods of Unmake sometimes develops a faint tremor, accompanied by a smell of cold and iron, and then a section of forest floor, anywhere from two to ten meters across, **sinks** — not collapses, but sinks smoothly, as if being drawn down from beneath. Anything standing on the sinking section goes with it.

What is known of the creature's appearance comes from the one recorded survivor, a Cult Weaver who fell into a Hunger Below depression and was pulled out by companions with ropes: she described an immense, flat form, like a manta ray the size of a building, made entirely of darkness and slow movement, its upper surface soft and adhesive, drawing everything that lands on it down into a fold in its body.

**Behavior:** Passive ambush. The Hunger Below feeds by waiting, perfectly still, just below the surface of the ground. It does not pursue. It does not rush. When something stands on it long enough, it simply... takes it.

**Abilities:** Effectively unlimited size within its subterranean habitat. **False ground** — indistinguishable from normal forest floor until the sinking begins. **Slow pull** — objects and people in contact with its surface are drawn down at a steady rate; a person with a rope anchor can resist for approximately ten minutes before the pull becomes irresistible.

**Weakness:** Salt in large quantities causes the Hunger Below to contract and retreat from the surface. Cult Weavers carry salt pouches when traversing areas known to contain these creatures.

**Lore Note:** There is some debate among Cerevane scholars about whether there is one Hunger Below or many. Some evidence suggests a single immense entity that ranges underground over a vast territory.

## 8. The Stitch-Faced

**Classification:** Unmade Humanoid (Cult-created)
**Threat Level:** High
**Behavior:** Dedicated tracker, near-relentless pursuit

**Appearance:** The Stitch-Faced are recognizably human in body — same height, same build, same proportions. But their faces have been *modified*. The features have been pulled apart and reassembled in a configuration that is not quite right — eyes too far apart or too close, nose removed and the space sewn shut with thick black thread, mouth stretched wide and fixed in place with more thread, cheeks pulled back to expose the jaw. The overall effect is deeply wrong in a way that is difficult to categorize but impossible to ignore.

**Behavior:** The Stitch-Faced are the Cult's created trackers — Unmade humans who have been specifically modified and conditioned to pursue and retrieve (not kill) targets that the Cult designates. They are relentless, tireless, and patient. They can track by more than scent — they sense the *emotional residue* left on environments by people who have passed through them, making them extremely difficult to evade.

**Abilities:** **Emotional tracking** (follows residue of strong feelings), **silence step** (they make no sound regardless of terrain), **paralytic touch** (a deliberate modification — they can transmit a Cult-designed paralytic through skin contact, allowing for capture), and **shadow step** (in darkness, they can move as a shadow for brief periods, emerging at a different shadow within line of sight).

**Weakness:** They are conditioned not to kill and will not do so even in self-defense. They are also, beneath the modification, still partially human — in documented cases, a Stitch-Faced has been halted by direct address using its pre-Cult name. This does not always work. But sometimes it does.

## 9. The Unmirror

**Classification:** Unmade Elemental (light/reflection)
**Threat Level:** Medium-High
**Behavior:** Mimicry, infiltration

**Appearance:** An Unmirror looks exactly like whatever is currently looking at it. Not an approximation — an exact, real-time reflection. To look at an Unmirror is to look at yourself, with one uncanny distinction: the reflection is always **slightly behind**. If you raise your hand, the Unmirror raises its hand approximately half a second later. If you run, it runs — a half second behind, maintaining the same distance.

When not actively mimicking, an Unmirror appears as a freestanding pool of absolute stillness — a vertical absence of visual information, roughly person-shaped, that simply shows nothing.

**Behavior:** The Unmirror is drawn to settlements and will attempt to infiltrate them by replacing a specific person. Once it has studied a target long enough (which can take anywhere from an hour to several days), it can produce a complete copy — appearance, voice, mannerisms, even smell — and will attempt to slip into the target's life while the original is quietly detained or killed.

**Abilities:** **Perfect mimicry** (after study), **delayed reflection** (in its natural state), **memory extraction** (physical contact with the Unmirror transmits the target's surface memories to the creature, accelerating its mimicry capability), and **twin confusion** (two Unmirrors in proximity can produce overlapping false copies of multiple people simultaneously).

**Weakness:** An Unmirror cannot mimic internal emotion convincingly. It can produce the right words and expressions, but people who know the original target well will often report a vague wrongness they can't quite articulate. Asking it about a strong emotional memory — a grief, a joy — will produce a slight delay and a response that is technically accurate but emotionally hollow. Fire reveals it instantly; its reflection does not waver as a normal mirror would.

---

## 10. The Cartographer's Curse

**Classification:** Unmade Conceptual Entity (spatial distortion)
**Threat Level:** Varies (Medium to Lethal, depending on exposure)
**Behavior:** Passive distortion field

**Appearance:** The Cartographer's Curse is not a creature in any conventional sense. It is a *place* — a section of the Woods of Unmake approximately half a mile in diameter where the fundamental spatial logic of the world has been disrupted. Inside the Curse, distances do not behave correctly. A tree that appears ten meters away may be two meters away or two kilometers. Walking north may bring you south, or may bring you somewhere that is not a direction. Paths double back on themselves. Familiar landmarks appear in impossible positions.

The Curse contains a visible center — a single large standing stone around which the distortion is most severe. The stone is covered in carvings that look like maps but map no terrain that has ever existed.

**Behavior:** The Cartographer's Curse does not pursue or attack. It simply exists, and its effects accumulate over time spent within it. Short exposures produce mild disorientation. Extended exposure produces spatial psychosis — a condition in which the affected person permanently loses the ability to perceive distance and direction correctly, leaving them unable to navigate any environment without assistance.

**Abilities:** **Spatial inversion**, **recursive looping** (repeated attempts to leave in the same direction reinforce the loop rather than breaking it), **map corruption** (any map brought within the Curse changes — the lines shift, the labels scramble, and the map becomes a representation of a space that does not exist), and **echo presence** (the Curse produces faint auditory and visual echoes of people who have been lost inside it).

**Weakness:** The only reliable method for traversing the Curse is to ignore all spatial perception entirely — close your eyes, choose a direction by feel, and walk in a perfectly straight line. Any attempt to navigate by sight or logic reinforces the distortion. A rope connecting all members of a party is essential.

**Lore Note:** The standing stone at the center is believed to be a Founder artifact — a navigation tool of some kind that was corrupted by the Unmaking. Some scholars believe it still functions, but for a spatial system that no longer corresponds to this world.

# PART FOUR: MAGICAL ARTIFACTS

The world of EXPAND is not rich in functional magic — the Unmaking consumes enchantments the same way it consumes everything else. The artifacts that remain are old, strange, and dangerous to use. Each one carries a cost.

---

## Artifact 1: The Stillheart

**Type:** Wearable (pendant, polished white stone on a chain of dull silver)
**Origin:** Founder-era, recovered from the ruins of Lorne
**Current Location:** In the private collection of a Cerevane noble family, though the family does not know what it is

**Appearance:** A flat oval of pale white stone, perfectly smooth, approximately the size of a thumb. The stone is warm to the touch regardless of ambient temperature. It has no inscriptions, markings, or visible signs of craftsmanship — it looks like a very smooth river stone — but it hums, very faintly, at a frequency just below the threshold of hearing.

**Properties:** The Stillheart is a fragment of a destroyed Stillstone, and it retains some of the original formation's function. While worn against the skin, it provides the wearer with partial resistance to Unmaking — shadow-threading effects are weakened against the wearer, Unmade creatures are instinctively averse to them (not afraid, but reluctant to approach), and the wearer's memories are protected against dissolution effects.

**Cost:** The Stillheart's protection has limits. When the wearer is exposed to a major Unmaking event — not a glancing encounter but a direct, sustained assault — the stone absorbs the damage. Each major absorption causes the stone to crack slightly. The stone can absorb approximately three major events before it shatters. When it shatters, the protection inverts briefly, and the wearer experiences all the damage the stone absorbed at once.

**Lore Note:** The Cult actively seeks this artifact and is aware of which family possesses it. The family has been protected from scrutiny so far by their political connections, but that protection is wearing thin.

## Artifact 2: The Book of Second Sight

**Type:** Tome (small, black leather cover, no title, 312 pages)
**Origin:** Compiled by a Cult Weaver named **Dresh the Unmoored** approximately 200 years before present
**Current Location:** Hidden in the lower archives of Cerevane's Library of Warranted Truths

**Appearance:** An unremarkable leather-bound book, slightly smaller than a standard codex, with pages made of a material that looks like vellum but feels wrong — slightly too warm, with a faint grain that runs the wrong direction. The text is written in standard language but in an ink that appears to be a very dark brown-red. The writing is very small and very precise.

**Properties:** The Book of Second Sight is a record of every shadow-threading session Dresh the Unmoored conducted over forty years of practice. Reading it does not simply provide information — it transmits Dresh's perceptions. A reader who studies a page carefully and concentrates will briefly experience what Dresh experienced during the threading described: the sensation of reaching past reality, the cold, the sounds from the other side, the things glimpsed there.

Used carefully, this makes the book an unparalleled source of intelligence about the Outer Dark, the nature of Unmaking, and the structure of the Cult's methods. A scholar who studied it seriously could learn things about the Cult's weaknesses and plans that no one else alive knows.

**Cost:** Every reading session that involves genuine immersion — actually experiencing Dresh's memories rather than just reading the words — leaves a faint residue in the reader's mind. Specifically, a small thread of Dresh's personality and memories integrates itself into the reader's consciousness. After several deep reading sessions, readers begin to notice intrusive thoughts in a voice that is not their own. After many sessions, the question of where the reader ends and Dresh begins becomes genuinely ambiguous.

## Artifact 3: The Lantern of Was

**Type:** Object (hand lantern, black iron frame, no visible fuel source)
**Origin:** Pre-Cult, possibly Founder-era
**Current Location:** In the possession of a wandering figure known as the Old Road Keeper, who walks the perimeter of the Woods of Unmake

**Appearance:** A standard hand lantern in construction, approximately 30 centimeters tall with a hinged door and a handle. The frame is black iron, not rusted but not polished either — it has the look of something very old that has been carefully maintained. Inside, where a candle or oil reservoir would be, there is nothing visible. But the lantern produces light — a soft, amber light that illuminates approximately ten meters in all directions.

**Properties:** The light of the Lantern of Was does not illuminate what *is* present — it illuminates what *was* present. Objects, people, structures, and paths that existed in the same location at some point in the past become faintly visible in the lantern's light: translucent, amber-colored, but detailed and clear. In the Woods of Unmake, this means the light shows what the forest looked like before the Unmaking — paths that have been consumed, clearings that no longer exist, and occasionally, people who were Unmade there.

This makes it an extraordinary navigation tool in the Woods — one can follow routes that the shadow consumed, confident that they were once passable. It can also reveal hidden rooms in old ruins, lost passages in buildings, and buried infrastructure.

**Cost:** The Lantern of Was shows the past, and the past shown is not always welcome. It does not filter its revelations for emotional content. Using it in certain locations will show horrible things: people in the moment of their Unmaking, or places in the moment of their destruction. Characters with existing trauma should be warned. Additionally, looking at something through the Lantern's light for too long begins to make the past seem more real than the present. Extended use risks **temporal anchor drift** — a condition in which the user begins to find the present increasingly unreliable and the past increasingly vivid.

## Artifact 4: The Null-Tongue

**Type:** Removable Object (a small, flat disc of black glass worn under the tongue)
**Origin:** Cult-made, approximately 150 years before present
**Current Location:** Multiple exist; one was recovered from a dead Cult Weaver and is now held by a Cerevane intelligence operative

**Appearance:** A flat disc of black glass, approximately the size of a small coin but thinner, with no markings. It is smooth on both sides and has a slightly concave shape designed to sit comfortably under the tongue.

**Properties:** When worn under the tongue, the Null-Tongue allows the user to speak in a resonance that is imperceptible to Unmade creatures — they cannot hear it. Speech, movement sounds, even the soft sounds of breathing are masked against Unmade perception. This does not make the user invisible to them, but any creature that hunts by sound (including many Unmade) will not register the user as present. Additionally, the Null-Tongue allows communication with other Null-Tongue wearers in a range of approximately 500 meters, regardless of intervening walls or terrain.

**Cost:** Each hour of sustained use causes the inside of the user's mouth to become slightly numb. After several hours, the numbness begins to affect speech — words become harder to form correctly, consonants slur. After extended use over days, the tongue itself becomes partially Unmade — still functional but now dark in color and, at its edges, slightly transparent. The change is irreversible without significant magical intervention.

## Artifact 5: The Witness Crown

**Type:** Wearable (crown, very thin dark metal, almost wire-like)
**Origin:** Unknown — does not match Founder aesthetics or Cult craftsmanship
**Current Location:** Displayed openly in the great hall of Cerevane's Assembly, as a historical curiosity. No one knows what it is.

**Appearance:** A very thin circlet of some metal that is almost, but not quite, black — it has a very faint iridescence in certain lights. It is extremely lightweight and bends easily; one would not think it could hold its shape on a head, but once placed, it sits perfectly. The metal is warm. It leaves no mark on the skin.

**Properties:** The Witness Crown provides its wearer with **perfect, instantaneous recall** of everything they have experienced since first wearing the Crown. Not improved memory — *perfect* recall, experienced as if the memory is happening in real-time. The wearer can replay any moment from their life with perfect fidelity: every word spoken, every detail of the environment, every sensation. They can also share memories directly with another person by holding their hands and concentrating.

**Cost:** The Crown preserves all memories equally and indefinitely, and the human mind was not built to carry this much weight. After wearing the Crown for several weeks, users begin to experience **memory overload** — unwanted, high-fidelity memories surfacing at random, triggered by the smallest sensory detail. An old conversation replays, crisp and complete, in the middle of a new one. The smell of bread brings back every meal the user has ever eaten simultaneously. With sustained use, the present becomes increasingly difficult to live in because the past is too loud.

**Lore Note:** There is a persistent, unverified rumor that the Crown has a deeper function that activates only when worn by someone who has already experienced a major Unmaking event — that for such a person, it does not merely preserve memories but allows them to *share* memories with the Unmade: to show a Hollowman its own past, to return to a Twice-Dead the moment they are trying to remember. If true, the Crown might be the most powerful anti-Cult tool in existence.

## NPC 1: Aldric Voss — Keeper of the Third Ward

**Role:** Ally (cautious), primary contact in Cerevane
**Alignment:** Survival-pragmatist; broadly on the side of preservation but not idealistic about it
**Location:** The Third Ward administrative building, Cerevane

### Physical Description

Aldric Voss is a man in his middle sixties who looks a decade older. He is tall but slightly stooped, with close-cropped grey hair and a short grey beard kept neat out of habit rather than vanity. His hands are large and calloused despite a desk career — he grew up on a working farm and the body remembers. He has a long scar running from his left ear to his jaw that he received during the War of Ash's final campaigns, when he served as a junior logistics officer. He wears the blue and grey of Cerevane's civic administration in a slightly worn, slightly loose version that suggests either modesty or exhaustion.

His most notable feature is his eyes — brown, attentive, and very still. He has the eyes of someone who has learned to watch everything without appearing to.

### Personality

Aldric is not warm. He is not cold, either — he is *careful*. Years of working in an institution that has been infiltrated by the Cult have taught him that warmth is a vulnerability and that easy trust is a gift the world rarely repays. He is direct, he keeps his word, and he will tell people unpleasant truths rather than comfortable lies — but he chooses who he tells anything to very slowly and very deliberately.

Beneath the caution is a man who is genuinely frightened — not for himself, but for the city. He has served Cerevane for forty years. He has watched it change, and not for the better. He has seen colleagues he respected turn out to be Cult agents. He has signed orders for the evacuation of neighborhoods consumed by the Expansion and watched people's faces as they understood they were losing everything. He does not have a great deal of hope left, and he conserves what remains carefully.

He has a daughter, **Mena**, who is a junior scholar at the Library of Warranted Truths. He talks about her rarely, because he is afraid of the Cult finding out how much she matters to him.

### Role in the Game

Aldric serves as the player's first serious contact in Cerevane — a person who knows enough to be useful and is cautious enough to test the player before sharing that knowledge. He is the source of several key quest chains and has access to intelligence about Cult activity in the city.

His arc involves a revelation: he has been unknowingly protecting a Cult agent embedded in his own department. When this comes to light, his response — whether he turns the agent in, confronts them alone, or, in the darkest timeline, tries to cover it up to protect his own reputation — depends heavily on choices the player has made in their relationship with him.

**Key Dialogue Hook:** *"The Cult doesn't recruit cowards. They recruit people who love something. Find out what a man loves, and you'll know if he can be turned. That's the most dangerous thing they taught me, and they didn't even know they were teaching it."*

## NPC 2: Seya of the Hollow Voice — First Mouth's Emissary

**Role:** Primary Antagonist (field agent), recurring presence
**Alignment:** Cult of Shadows, true believer
**Location:** Mobile; active throughout the Remnant, including deep in Cerevane

### Physical Description

Seya is a woman of indeterminate age — somewhere between thirty and fifty, with a face that seems to resist being clocked precisely, as if the specific details of her appearance keep reorganizing themselves. She is of medium height and slim, and she dresses in the grey-brown of a traveler or a merchant, deliberately unremarkable. Her hair is black and straight, usually worn in a practical braid. Her left hand is missing its ring and middle fingers — a shadow-threading injury she refers to only as "a bargain well made."

Her voice is the thing people remember. It is quiet, controlled, and carries without effort, and it has a quality that is not quite an accent but is not quite not an accent either — something slightly off in the vowels and the rhythm. When she wants to, it is extremely pleasant to listen to. When she doesn't want to be pleasant, it is something else entirely.

### Personality

Seya is genuinely one of the most intelligent people in the Remnant, and she knows it, and she does not find this comforting. She would prefer to be wrong more often. Being right has cost her many things she valued.

She is a true believer in the Cult's theology — she genuinely believes the Grand Expansion will free rather than destroy, and she approaches her work with the focused dedication of someone doing what they believe to be right. This makes her more dangerous than a cynical agent, not less. She will not take bribes. She cannot be flattered off her mission. She does not have obvious self-interest to exploit.

What she *does* have is doubt. Not about the Expansion itself, but about Veleth the Unfinished's methods. Seya has seen things she was not supposed to see: Cult operations that did not look like liberation to her, changes in Veleth that do not match the theology Seya committed herself to. She buries this doubt under discipline and loyalty, but it is there, and a sufficiently observant player who builds the right kind of relationship with her may find a crack in the wall.

She is not redeemable in any simple sense. She has done terrible things and knows it. But she is not *unreachable*.

### Role in the Game

Seya is the primary antagonist the player actually speaks to — Veleth the Unfinished operates at a remove, but Seya is on the ground, pursuing the Cult's agenda in Cerevane. She and the player will cross paths repeatedly, and the nature of those encounters is shaped by the player's choices.

She is also a source of information, if approached correctly. She knows things about the Cult that Aldric doesn't, and there are moments where she will share them — not out of incompetence, but because she has decided it serves her purposes, or because some remnant of her pre-Cult self surfaces unexpectedly.

Her arc culminates in a confrontation that gives the player a genuine choice about her fate — and that choice has meaningful consequences regardless of which way it goes.

**Key Dialogue Hook:** *"You think you're trying to save the world. That's touching. We thought so too, once — that there was something to save. Then we looked past the edge of it and understood: what you're saving is the cage. We're trying to open the door."*

## NPC 3: Orren Vael — The Cartographer Who Came Back

**Role:** Wildcard, potential ally or complication
**Alignment:** Unknown; self-described as "post-alignment"
**Location:** A small workshop in Cerevane's lower city; occasionally the Woods of Unmake

### Physical Description

Orren Vael is forty-two years old and looks it, and also looks as if the forty-two years were particularly eventful. He is of medium build with a slight, persistent tremor in his right hand that he ignores completely, as if it belongs to someone else. His hair has gone fully white — not gradually grey but shockingly white, the kind of white that happens suddenly. His eyes are a very pale grey that in certain lights appears to have a faint amber tint.

He dresses comfortably and without much attention, and his workshop is covered in maps — pinned to every wall, rolled in tubes, stacked in piles, spread flat on every horizontal surface. Most of them are of the Woods of Unmake. Most of them have areas marked in red ink. He is not the first cartographer to map the Woods; he is, as far as anyone knows, the only one to have spent three years inside them, returned, and retained most of his sanity.

His left forearm, from wrist to elbow, is Unmade — the skin is black-grey and slightly translucent, the bones visible through it, and it does not respond to cold or pain. He keeps it covered with a leather brace he made himself.

### Personality

Orren spent three years in the Woods of Unmake. He has seen things. He has also, technically, *been* some of those things, briefly, before pulling himself back. This has given him a perspective on the Expansion and the Unmade that is neither the terror of an ordinary person nor the enthusiasm of a Cult member, but something more clinical, more honest, and considerably more strange.

He is cheerful in a way that unsettles people — not the cheerfulness of someone who has not seen hard things, but the cheerfulness of someone who has decided that seeing hard things does not have to foreclose enjoyment of small ones. He likes tea. He likes making maps. He is very, very good at both.

He does not fully trust himself. The partial Unmaking of his arm was not the only thing that changed during his time in the Woods — he is aware that his perceptions are not entirely reliable, that there is a small but non-zero part of him that is oriented toward the darkness rather than away from it. He monitors this with a mixture of vigilance and matter-of-factness.

He is the best source in the game for practical intelligence about the Woods of Unmake — he has mapped most of it and knows the habits of its creatures, the locations of its Cult installations, and the safest routes through. He will share this information without excessive drama, for reasonable payment or for people he decides he likes.

### Role in the Game

Orren is the game's primary practical expert — the person who knows *how* things work in the Woods, who can prepare a player character for specific encounters, who has resources (maps, equipment, contacts among the communities on the edge of the Unmade) that are available to no one else.

He is also a character with a genuine secret: during his three years in the Woods, he made contact with something in the Outer Dark — not the Cult's version of it, but a direct, unmediated contact with whatever the darkness actually is. What he learned from that contact does not entirely match the Cult's theology, and it does not entirely match Cerevane's official view either. What he learned is something the player will need to decide how to use.

His arc involves the question of his arm — the Unmaking is slowly progressing, and at some point he will have to either accept further help or accept what it means if he doesn't.

**Key Dialogue Hook:** *"The Cult will tell you the dark wants to free you. The scholars in Cerevane will tell you the dark wants to erase you. They're both right and they're both missing the point. The dark doesn't want anything. It just is. The wanting is something we bring to it. That should probably be reassuring and somehow never is."*
